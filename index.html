San Francisco’s public-facing art: A legacy of protest, community and resistance

By: Libby Raymond, Eleni Tecos, Eliza Tucker

First Subheading

Stepping into the heart of San Francisco’s Mission District, one can not help but notice the murals lining the streets. Some sun-faded and battered by years of weather, others still vibrant and exploding with colors, the murals extend above storefronts, down alleys and across the garage doors of homes. What they all have in common are the stories they tell of culture, pain and resistance in the local community.

Public-facing arts — murals, mosaics and other works on the streets and buildings of San Francisco — have long served as a canvas for political expression, beginning in the 1970s with immigrant communities protesting U.S. involvement in Central America by painting community murals in the Mission District. Today, public-facing arts continue to address injustice, with many artists calling attention to the genocide in Gaza. From the Mission District’s earliest murals to today’s urgent cries for justice, public-facing art is a compelling tool used to fight back against injustice.

One of the hallmark institutions of public-facing arts of San Francisco is Precita Eyes Muralists, a community-based, inner-city mural arts organization dedicated to mural creation, preservation and education. Founded in 1977, Precita Eyes offers walking tours through some of the most famous murals in the Mission.

Second Subheading

The murals of the Mission have a rich history dating back to the 1970s, when the first pieces began popping up in Balmy Alley – regarded by long-time muralist and Precita Eyes tour guide Patricia Rose as the original mural alley that has gone on to inspire countless others. Many of the earliest murals addressed political strife in Central American countries, as civil wars in El Salvador, Guatemala and Nicaragua prompted people to immigrate to San Francisco. Each minute detail of the murals in Balmy Alley serves as a reminder of a collective history of repression and retaliation that refuses to be forgotten.

“I’ve been doing this for, I don’t know, 50 years or more. And, unfortunately, these issues don’t seem to go away,” said Juana Alicia, a long-time artist and activist instrumental in the Chicano Art Movement and most known for her Las Lechugueras, Ceasefire/Alto Al Fuego and MAESTRAPEACE.

Alicia, whose murals can be seen in California, Pennsylvania, Nicaragua and Mexico, where she is now located, often draws inspiration from current events. By working through intersectional lenses such as decolonization, feminism and indigenous identity, Alicia sees her art as a way to confront issues within the communities that surround her.

“We have to spend our energy carefully,” said Alicia. “For me, art – my artwork – is the way that I can deal with the political repression that we’ve suffered throughout the history of the United States – against women, against people of color, against immigrants, against non-binary people, against gay people, against disabled people.”

Third Subheading

Public-facing arts continue to act as powerful tools of protest, with shifting — yet still urgent —themes. Recently, San Francisco has hosted a new wave of art that challenges the ongoing genocide in Gaza.

Artists like Dottie Lux have turned to using their art to express the pain and outrage felt by Palestinians. In her work, Lux uses her platform to challenge the status quo, even if it means stepping away from the commercial success may bring her.

“For me, it really boils down to two things: whether you want to be willing to use your art to say something, or if you’re fully relying on your art to make money,” said Lux, an activist and artist who recently curated a multi-sensory exhibit transforming art into essential funds for families in Gaza. “I have been a performance artist for over 20 years, and for most of my career, I’ve used my voice to be cheeky or funny or appealing – very commercial. But since October of 2023, I didn’t really feel like I had that luxury anymore.” Lux spent three months in the West Bank in 2024, serving as a protective presence with Palestinian families on behalf of the International Solidarity Movement in Palestine. Her time there greatly influenced her exhibit, which featured photography, paintings, a soundscape and more, allowing visitors a varied understanding of the brutal realities of colonization.

Fourth Subheading

“We are doing the very most that we possibly can, and it is still the least that we can do,” said Dottie Lux, an activist and artist who recently curated a multi-sensory exhibit transforming art into essential funds for families in Gaza.

Another artist, Palestinian-American Chris Gazaleh, has been creating art that raises awareness about the history, people and struggle for freedom in Palestine for years, beginning with graffiti letters in his youth and later moving to mural arts at 21 years old. His practice has earned him a wall in Clarion Alley, which has been his practice since 2012.
